Jazz it up with Genres!

Jazz is a great genre with a lot of variety throughout the decades, explore this timeline and all the genres that came with it!

Timeline:

Late 1890s-1910s: Ragtime

  • The forerunner of Jazz, started it all.
  • Influenced by minstrel-show songs, African American banjo styles, off-beat dance rhythms, and elements of European music.
  • Played solely on the piano
  • Music was often characterized with an accented left-hand beat opposed with a fast, syncopated melody in the right hand
  • Notable artists: Ernest Hogan, Scott Joplin
    • Latter often referred to as the King of Ragtime, with popular music such as 'Maple Leaf Rag' and 'The Entertainer.'

1920s: Early Jazz / New Orleans

  • Was influenced by and evolved from marching bands, ragtime, and Afro-Latin rhythms.
    • Influence from Afro Latin rhythms from places like Cuba was especially notable, with rhythms like the tresillo rhythm being prominent.
  • Instrumentation included drums, trumpets, trombone, saxophone, and more.
  • Had collective and solo improvisation, a departure from traditional bands of the time.
    • The start of improvisation being a main theme within jazz genres.
  • In Chicago epecially, the music was becoming more 'soulful' and vocal-orientated.
  • Mainly existed in speakeasies, brothels and in the darker parts of society as a result of prohibition and racial segregation.
  • Notable Artists: Jellyroll Morton, Louis Armstrong, Buddy Bolden.

1930s: Swing and Big Band

  • The lessening rules of segregation and the end of prohibition resulted in jazz becoming way more mainstream.
  • Large big band composers started to create jazz standards with their music.
  • Many soloists and vocalists also emerged in this period.
  • Instruments simular to early jazz and were as followed:
    • Piano, bass and drums for rhythm.
    • Trumpets and trombones as a brass section.
    • Saxophones and clarinet as wind instuments.
    • Occasionally, there were string instruments like the violin and the guitar.
  • Music was mainly played for dancers in bars and such.
  • Notable Artists: Duke Ellington, Count Basie and Benny Goodman.

1940s: Bebop

  • Evolved from swing and other past jazz genres with a focus on higher tempos and more complex chord changes
  • The concepts of improvisation were finally being really fine-tuned and focused on.
  • More focus on technical facility, syncopation and complex melodic phrasing rather than vocal arrangements and lyrical melodies
  • Rhythm section began to play broken and syncopated rhythms for a more expressive interaction with their accompaniment.
    • Smaller ensembles that used the rhythm section in new ways were popular
    • Big band music had a steady pumping quarter note swing feel to drive the the larger ensemble.
  • This phase of jazz started by the younger generation and was part of the 'hipster' culture.
  • Instruments were the same as swing
  • Notable Artists: Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie.

Late 1940s-1950s: Cool Jazz

  • As a result of WW2 jazz began to adopt a more relaxed tone and tempo with more formal arrangements.
    • Even took on more elements of classical music
  • Improvisers within the genre incorperated more tonal textures and colour that contributed to this tonal shift.
    • A departure from Bebop which was very focused on virtuosity and improvisation.
  • Instruments often found in swing were still used, but some started to play with more unusual jazz instuments such as French horns, flutes, tuba, cello, and vibraphones.
  • Notable artists: Coleman Hawkins and Bix Beiderbecke.

Mid 1950s: Hard bop

  • Returned back to fast tempos, blending bebop with inspiration from blues, bebop, and gospel music.
    • Had a funkier sound with simpler melodies that strongly referenced the blues
    • Many think this move to blues was a conscious decision against Cool Jazz and a return to a more Afrocentric sound.
  • Used the instruments often used in the past like the piano, trumpet, saxophone, with a double bass/drum rhythm section
    • Small lineup with the soloist doing improvisation.
  • Notable artists: Lee Morgan and Miles Davis.

Late 1950s-1960s: Modal Jazz

  • Centered around musical scales and using them in ways that were unique and different from traditional Western harmony.
    • Western harmony relies upon a tonal key center with related chords and cadences; it changes chords frequently.
    • Modal harmony, however, will shift modes/scales; it will use a scale longer and then change to a different one. Basically, you go through all the scales in one chord rather then shifting quickly through chords like in Traditional Western music.
  • Also had great emphasis on improvisation, as artists had more time on chords to create interesting sounds as a result of more time going through all the scales/modes in a chord.
  • Instruments like the ones seen before: please see Swing and Big Band for the instruments if further expanation needed.
  • Notable artists: George Russell and Miles Davis.

Late 1960s-1970s: Avant-Garde and Fusion

  • When jazz started to use electronic instruments, and began to stretch the boundries of what jazz is.
    • The use of atonal and arrhythmic concepts from classical music broadened the scope of jazz.
  • A large want to break convention among artists of the time
    • Denied the rules of traditional jazz that were restrictive, such as harmony and chord changes, regular tempos, and compositional forms.
  • Avant-Garde was especially and proudly Afrocentric, linking back to the civil rights movement at the time.
  • Along with the instruments common in jazz, use of electric guitar/bass or electric organ/piano became more commonplace.
  • Notable artists: Herbie Hancock and Chick Corea.

1980s-Present: Modern Jazz

  • Incorporates elements from swing, bebop, modal jazz, and other styles, blurring genre boundaries.
    • Has nonfunctional harmonies, intricate melodies, odd-time signatures and a demand for skill in musicians.
  • Draws a lot of classical music and non-western cultures.
  • Use of instruments used in the genres that inspiration was drawn from
    • Alto/Tenor saxophone, Trombone, Trumpet, Double bass, Piano, Clarinet, Drum Kit, Bassoon, Steelpan, and Acoustic-electric guitar to name a few.
  • Notable artists: Bill Frisell and Avishai Cohen.
